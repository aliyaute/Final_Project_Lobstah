China turns to Canada as US lobster market declines

Gulf of Maine is the only source of the American Lobster, but while Maine loses its revenue due to tariffs, Canada comes out as the winner in the Trade War

Aliya Uteuova

Published December 14, 2019

Lobster landings in Maine have increased steadily in the last six decades, in 2018 a record-breaking 250 millions of pounds of lobster was harvested. However, many lobstermen remember the years when the catch, and therefore the salary was under. Still, they were hopeful that the lobster population, directly dependent on climate, would recover. But today, despite the landings at an all time high, a different force is threatening the livelihood of Maine fisheries.

In July 2018, China, the largest market outside of North American for the Maine lobster, imposed 25 percent punitive tariffs on goods coming form the United States. The Maine lobster, an industry worth 1.4 billion dollars, is among the goods that is being hit with retaliatory tariffs.

Overwhelmingly reliant on the lobster export, Maine lost nearly 85 percent of its usual export to the Chinese market in 2018. In 2017, the US exported nearly 12 million pounds of live lobster, but a year after the tariffs, the number of exports declined by nearly 10 million pounds.The loss of business soon translated into the loss of jobs. Dealers like the Lobster Co., of Arundel laid off half of the wholesale staff.

In September 2017, the Canadians and the Europeans negotiated a comprehensive economic trade agreement, which eliminated tariffs imposed lobster coming from Canada to the EU. This preferential trade agreement has already been in effect when the retaliatory tariffs by China were announced a year later.

The peak in the lobster landings happens in May, June, and December. That is why Maine and Canada trade with each other to make up for the demand in off seasons

“Not only our trade with China is down, our trade with Europe is also down by 20 percent of our historical high,” Annie Tselikis, executive director of the Maine Lobster Dealers' Association, says. And today, Canada is trading with China at an all time high, exempt from punitive tariffs that the US is experiencing. In June 2018 alone, Canada exported 33 million pounds of live lobster to China. In all of 2018, the US exported 2.2 million pounds of the same species.

This forced the lobster dealers to re-route their goods to Canada, the dominant exporter of American lobster today. Canada is the obvious winner in the US-China Trade War and the climate change, which is forcing lobster to swim northward, is playing into Canadian fishermen's favour
